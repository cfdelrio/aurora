# The Athlete Model — Conceptual Discovery

> What must Aurora know about an athlete in order to correctly interpret the impact of a workout?
>
> Not everything that can be measured. Only what changes the meaning of training.

---

## How to Read This Document

Same discipline as prior discoveries. Every substantive statement is tagged.

| Tag | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **[FACT]** | Well-established or self-evidently true. |
| **[HYPOTHESIS]** | Reasoned but unproven for our athletes. Must be validated. |
| **[ASSUMPTION]** | A stance Aurora chooses. Not a truth — a commitment. |
| **[QUESTION]** | Open question to resolve before designing. |
| **[UNKNOWN]** | We genuinely don't know. |

---

## Central Insight

[FACT] A workout has no intrinsic impact.

Impact emerges from the interaction between:

- a specific workout
- a specific athlete
- at a specific moment in time
- pursuing a specific purpose

Therefore: **The athlete is not merely the receiver of training. The athlete is the context through which training acquires meaning.**

The same 60-minute threshold session produces wildly different impact depending on:

- Whether the athlete is building toward a goal or in maintenance
- Whether they're fresh or already fatigued
- Whether they've done threshold work before or it's novel stimulus
- Whether they have capacity for adaptation or are limited by injury
- Whether external stress is high or low
- Whether they're entering peak season or base season

Aurora cannot interpret the workout without interpreting the athlete.

## Discovery Area 1: Identity

### Definition

[FACT] The relatively stable characteristics that define an athlete independently of their current training state. Identity answers: "Who is this athlete, separate from today?"

### What It Includes

**Sport and Discipline**
- Bike, run, swim, triathlon, or multi-sport
- Specialization within sport (sprinter vs. endurance, road vs. mountain, etc.)
- [FACT] Sport determines what capacities matter, which constraints apply, what success looks like

**Physiology Archetype**
- [HYPOTHESIS] Each athlete has a fundamental physiology (metabolic type, muscle fiber distribution, thermoregulation pattern) that rarely changes
- Examples: natural sprinter, natural endurance athlete, natural climber
- [ASSUMPTION] This is not immutable ceiling but rather the direction of easiest adaptation

**Age**
- [FACT] Age determines recovery speed, adaptation response, injury risk, realistic capacity ceiling
- [ASSUMPTION] Age is more than a number; it's a phase of athletic life (developing, peak, veteran, veteran-plus)

**Sex**
- [FACT] Affects physiology, recovery patterns, hormonal response to stimulus
- [ASSUMPTION] Sex should be known but not prescriptive; individual variation exceeds group difference

**Competitive Category**
- Pro, age-group, recreational, developmental
- [FACT] Category affects what risks are acceptable, what standards apply, what effort is appropriate

**Experience in Sport**
- [FACT] Years practicing changes how the athlete responds to stimulus
- New athletes: larger adaptation response to stimulus, more rapid gains, more susceptible to overtraining
- Experienced athletes: smaller gains, higher capacity for load, more resistant to disruption

**Competitive History**
- [HYPOTHESIS] Athlete's previous achievements and defeats shape how they respond to similar future situations
- [QUESTION] Is competitive history identity (permanently part of who they are) or merely historical context?

### What It Is Not

- Not their current fitness level (fitness changes; identity persists)
- Not their current motivation (motivation fluctuates; core identity endures)
- Not their current capability in any single domain (capabilities change; sport identity is stable)
- Not their role in a team (if applicable, team role can shift; core identity is stable)

### Examples

- "This athlete is a 34-year-old male triathlete with 12 years of racing experience, natural endurance physiology, age-group competitive category."
- "This athlete is a 23-year-old female runner, elite category, predominantly slow-twitch physiology, 6 years in sport."

### Anti-Examples

- "This athlete is fit" (that's current state/capacity, not identity)
- "This athlete is motivated" (that's state, not identity)
- "This athlete is a climber" (that's a specialty within sport, which can shift)

### Open Questions

- [QUESTION] Does an athlete's sport change their identity, or does their identity determine their sport?
- [QUESTION] Can an athlete switch sports and maintain core identity, or does sport shift identity?
- [QUESTION] How much of "identity" is chosen (I decided to be an endurance athlete) vs. discovered (my physiology is suited to endurance)?

## Discovery Area 2: Purpose

### Definition

[FACT] Why the athlete is training right now. Purpose is what gives meaning to effort. Without purpose, training is activity; with purpose, activity becomes training.

### What It Includes

**Current Competitive Objective**
- Specific event or race the athlete is preparing for
- [FACT] This is the near-term anchor (weeks to months)
- Examples: "National Championships on July 15", "Local 5K race in 4 weeks", "Season opener in 10 weeks"

**Training Phase Purpose**
- The specific reason for this block of training
- Build (building capacity in a domain)
- Peak (tapering toward competition)
- Maintenance (sustaining without advancement)
- Recovery (restoring readiness after fatigue or injury)
- Transition (moving between phases or seasons)
- [FACT] Phase purpose determines whether a workout should tax the system or spare it

**Development Purpose**
- What capacity is the athlete trying to build?
- Examples: "Improving aerobic base", "Building strength", "Developing tactical racing ability"
- [FACT] The same workout means different things if the athlete is developing aerobic vs. anaerobic vs. technique

**Longer-Term Vision**
- Where is the athlete trying to go?
- Examples: "Transition from sprinter to endurance athlete (18 months)", "Build professional racing capability (2 years)", "Return to racing after injury (6 months)"
- [HYPOTHESIS] Long-term vision influences how impact is interpreted even for short-term workouts

**Why Training Matters (Existential Purpose)**
- Is this professional livelihood? Recreation? Self-expression? Health? Competition? Mastery?
- [ASSUMPTION] The deeper "why" affects how much risk is acceptable, how persistent the athlete is when things get hard, what counts as success

### What It Is Not

- Not a vague aspiration ("be a better athlete")
- Not a metric ("get stronger")
- Not a feeling ("I want to feel like a racer")
- Not something that lasts forever (purpose is time-bound)

### Examples

- "Purpose: Prepare for Ironman in 10 weeks (near-term). Build endurance capacity in running (phase purpose). Transition to triathlon as primary sport (longer-term). Racing is livelihood."
- "Purpose: Recover readiness over 4-week transition block. Build foundational aerobic base for upcoming season. Non-professional, pursuing mastery."

### Anti-Examples

- "Get faster" (too vague, no anchor)
- "Be stronger" (not a purpose, just a hoped-for outcome)
- "Train hard" (that's effort, not purpose)

### Open Questions

- [QUESTION] Does purpose ever stay static, or is it always shifting? Should Aurora assume purpose is fixed or fluid?
- [QUESTION] When athlete's stated purpose conflicts with their behavior (says they want to peak but keeps doing base training), which is true?
- [QUESTION] How does Aurora distinguish between purpose (what the athlete is trying to do) and goal (what they hope will happen)?
- [QUESTION] Should Aurora ever challenge an athlete's stated purpose, or accept it as given?

## Discovery Area 3: Current State

### Definition

[FACT] The athlete's measurable condition right now. Current state changes moment-to-moment, day-to-day. It is temporary, dynamic, and includes both objective and subjective dimensions.

### What It Includes

**Acute Fatigue**
- Accumulated stress from the past week
- [FACT] Measured indirectly: resting heart rate, heart rate variability, training completion, performance in key sessions
- Affects readiness for another hard effort

**Chronic Fatigue**
- Accumulated stress from the past 4+ weeks
- [FACT] Measured indirectly: trend in power output, trend in recovery metrics, susceptibility to minor illness
- Affects adaptation capacity

**Recovery Status**
- Days since last hard effort
- Sleep quality and quantity (if measurable)
- Perceived recovery (if reported)
- [FACT] Recovery status determines whether another stimulus will build or break

**Readiness**
- Likelihood the athlete will perform well in a maximal or high-intensity effort
- [FACT] Readiness is the intersection of recovery, acute fatigue, and confidence
- Measured indirectly through power output, HR response, workout completion

**Confidence**
- Belief in their capability
- Belief in the program
- Belief they can execute the plan
- [FACT] Confidence affects effort, risk tolerance, willingness to push when hurt
- [HYPOTHESIS] Confidence is as important as physiology for performance

**Motivation**
- Intrinsic drive to train
- Extrinsic motivation (race coming up, peers training)
- [FACT] Motivation affects compliance, intensity of effort, willingness to endure discomfort
- [ASSUMPTION] Motivation fluctuates; the athlete's job is to train anyway

**Injury and Soreness**
- Active injuries (limiting movement)
- Soreness (limiting intensity)
- Areas of vulnerability
- [FACT] Injury state determines what stimulus is safe and what stimulus is dangerous

**External Stress**
- Work stress
- Relationship stress
- Financial stress
- Health issues (illness, inflammation)
- [FACT] High external stress reduces recovery capacity, increases injury risk, reduces mental resilience

### What It Is Not

- Not stable or permanent (state changes; identity endures)
- Not destiny (bad current state is not predictive; it's reversible)
- Not the athlete's fault ("I'm fatigued" is a state, not a character flaw)
- Not purely physiological (psychology is part of state)

### Examples

- "Current State: Acute fatigue moderate. Chronic fatigue low. Recovery adequate (60 hours since hard session). Readiness: medium-high. Confidence: high. Motivation: high. No active injuries. External stress: low."
- "Current State: Acute fatigue high. Chronic fatigue moderate-high. Recovery poor (32 hours since hard session, slept 5 hours, work stress elevated). Readiness: low. Confidence: shaken by yesterday's failed effort. Motivation: low. Mild knee soreness. External stress: high."

### Anti-Examples

- "The athlete is tired" (too vague; which dimension?)
- "The athlete is recovering" (recovery is process; state is snapshot)
- "The athlete is fit" (fitness is capacity; state is condition)

### Open Questions

- [QUESTION] How much of state is objective (measurable in data) vs. subjective (athlete's report)? Can Aurora claim state from data alone?
- [QUESTION] Does current state predict response to training, or is current state response to training?
- [QUESTION] How quickly does state change? Is a state measured today still true tomorrow?
- [QUESTION] Can Aurora see state when the athlete cannot? (E.g., "you're more fatigued than you realize")

## Discovery Area 4: Capacity

### Definition

[FACT] The athlete's capability in specific domains. Capacities change slowly (weeks to months) compared to state (days). Capacity is "what the athlete can do"; state is "what the athlete can do right now."

### What It Includes

**Aerobic Capacity**
- VO2Max (absolute and relative)
- Lactate Threshold Power (FTP or FTHR)
- Sustainable power at various zones
- [FACT] Aerobic capacity is the foundation for endurance sports
- [HYPOTHESIS] Aerobic capacity responds to threshold and sweet-spot stimulus

**Anaerobic Capacity**
- Peak power (5-second effort)
- Anaerobic threshold (1-minute effort)
- Power at VO2Max (3-5 minute effort)
- [HYPOTHESIS] Anaerobic capacity responds to interval stimulus

**Muscular Strength**
- Force production in key movements
- Strength endurance (force sustained over time)
- [FACT] Strength is prerequisite for power; power is prerequisite for speed

**Technical Proficiency**
- Economy of movement (efficiency at given effort)
- Skill execution (technique in sport-specific movements)
- [HYPOTHESIS] Technique develops slowly and is hard to undo; a trained motor pattern persists
- [FACT] Technique matters more as effort increases (poor technique breaks down under stress)

**Tactical and Mental Capability**
- Racing IQ (ability to execute strategy)
- Decision-making under pressure
- Resilience under discomfort
- [HYPOTHESIS] Mental capacity develops through experience; difficult to measure directly
- [UNKNOWN] How much of mental capacity is trainable vs. trait

**Adaptability**
- How much stimulus the athlete can handle and recover from
- Individual lag (time between stimulus and visible response)
- Magnitude of response (does 1 unit of stimulus produce 1 unit of adaptation?)
- [HYPOTHESIS] Each athlete has an individual response pattern

### Relationships to Other Concepts

**Capacity vs. State**
- Capacity: "This athlete can sustain 280W for 40 minutes" (trainable ability)
- State: "This athlete can sustain 280W right now" (current condition)
- An athlete with high capacity might be in low-readiness state (fresh, but hasn't trained it recently)

**Capacity vs. Identity**
- Identity includes physiology archetype (natural sprinter, endurance type)
- Capacity is the expression of that archetype right now
- A natural sprinter who has trained specifically for endurance will develop endurance capacity, but their physiology archetype hasn't changed

### Examples

- "Capacity: VO2Max 58 mL/kg/min, FTP 320W, anaerobic peak 1,850W, highly economical in steady-state, tactical maturity high, adaptability very high (responds quickly and dramatically to stimulus)."
- "Capacity: VO2Max 42 mL/kg/min, FTP 210W, anaerobic peak 950W, less economical (energetically expensive technique), tactical maturity developing, adaptability slow (large stimulus needed for visible response)."

### Anti-Examples

- "This athlete is strong" (that's a vague assessment; what capacity?)
- "This athlete can go hard" (readiness, not capacity)
- "This athlete is talented" (identity/archetype, not current capacity)

### Open Questions

- [QUESTION] Can Aurora measure capacity directly, or only infer it from state and workout performance?
- [QUESTION] Is capacity the same as fitness? Or is fitness something else?
- [QUESTION] How does Aurora distinguish between "capacity hasn't developed" and "capacity exists but isn't being expressed due to state"?
- [QUESTION] Can an athlete lose capacity, or is capacity retained (dormant) once developed?

## Discovery Area 5: History

### Definition

[FACT] The record of what the athlete has done, experienced, learned, and overcome. History is not identity, but it shapes how the athlete responds to future stimulus.

### What It Includes

**Personal Records and Achievements**
- Best performances in key efforts
- Breakthrough moments (when something clicked)
- [FACT] Past achievements are reference points; they prove what's possible

**Previous Training Patterns**
- What has worked before (and in what context)
- What hasn't worked
- Learned responses ("I know I don't tolerate high volume")
- [FACT] History teaches the athlete what stimulus produces what response for them

**Previous Overtraining or Injury Episodes**
- When the athlete broke down and why
- How they recovered
- [FACT] These moments permanently sensitize the athlete; they change risk tolerance going forward
- [ASSUMPTION] An athlete with history of overtraining will be more cautious about load

**Setbacks and Failures**
- Races that didn't go well
- Efforts that failed
- Competitions where the athlete underperformed
- [HYPOTHESIS] Setbacks shape confidence and affect response to similar future situations

**Coaching Relationships**
- Who has coached them before
- What that coach believed and how the athlete responded
- [FACT] Previous coaching shapes the athlete's beliefs about training

**Accumulated Wisdom**
- Lessons learned (what works, what doesn't, what conditions matter)
- Mental landmarks (moments that changed their understanding)
- [ASSUMPTION] A wise athlete trusts their own experience

### Why It Matters

[FACT] An athlete with 10 years of history is not the same as a new athlete with the same current capacity. The experienced athlete:

- Knows what stimulus produces what response in their body
- Has learned their own patterns and limits
- Carries confidence from past victories
- Carries caution from past failures
- Has developed wisdom about what works
- Can evaluate advice critically rather than blindly follow it

### What It Is Not

- Not the athlete's identity (history is accumulated; identity is stable)
- Not their current capability (history shows what was; capacity shows what is)
- Not their purpose (purpose is forward-looking; history is backward-looking)
- Not deterministic (history explains patterns; it doesn't predict the future)

### Examples

- "History: 8 years in sport. PR of 280W FTP, set 2 years ago. Has overreached twice (both times at high volume + intensity without recovery). Thrives on polarized training but struggles with moderate-intensity blocks. Recovered well from injury 3 years ago, now cautious about load. Previous coach was metrics-focused; current coach values feel and adaptation."
- "History: 2 years in sport. Steady progression, no major setbacks. No overtraining episodes. PRs improving consistently. Naive to load management; has never been broken down by training."

### Anti-Examples

- "This athlete is experienced" (vague; experienced at what?)
- "This athlete has failed before" (true, but what does that mean for future training?)

### Open Questions

- [QUESTION] How much should Aurora weight history vs. current data? If history says "polarized works" but current data shows the athlete adapting to moderate intensity, which is true?
- [QUESTION] Can an athlete unlearn a pattern? Or does history permanently shape response?
- [QUESTION] When should Aurora override history because the athlete has changed?
- [QUESTION] How does Aurora account for coaching changes (same athlete, different coach, different results)?

## Discovery Area 6: Constraints

### Definition

[FACT] The limits on adaptation, intensity, recovery, and risk that the athlete carries. Constraints are not excuses; they are reality conditions that shape what training is possible.

### What It Includes

**Injury History and Current Vulnerabilities**
- Previous injuries that healed but left lasting sensitivity
- Current structural limitations (bad knees, weak shoulders)
- Movement patterns that are unsafe under fatigue
- [FACT] An injured athlete has different adaptation potential than a healthy one
- [ASSUMPTION] Injury is not weakness; it's constraint that must be honored

**Age**
- Younger athletes: rapid adaptation, but injury risk when overloaded, longer recovery from large efforts
- Older athletes: slower adaptation, more time needed between hard efforts, higher injury risk under fatigue
- [FACT] Age is not a ceiling; it's a rate modifier

**Available Training Time**
- Hours per week the athlete can allocate to training
- [FACT] An athlete with 5 hours per week cannot follow a 15-hour-per-week plan
- [ASSUMPTION] Constraint on time forces prioritization (which efforts matter most)

**Sleep Availability**
- How much sleep the athlete can actually get
- [FACT] Sleep is where adaptation happens; without sufficient sleep, stimulus is waste
- [ASSUMPTION] An athlete with 5 hours of sleep per night has fundamentally different adaptation potential than one with 8 hours

**External Stress**
- Work demands
- Family/relationship demands
- Financial stress
- Health challenges
- [FACT] External stress reduces recovery capacity and increases injury risk
- [ASSUMPTION] An athlete under high external stress needs lower training load than one at low stress

**Genetics/Physiology Ceiling**
- Some capacities have genetic limits
- An athlete with low VO2Max potential will not become an endurance powerhouse regardless of training
- [HYPOTHESIS] Genetic ceiling matters more for elite-level competition; for most athletes, training and consistency matter more than genetics

**Access Constraints**
- Training facilities available
- Equipment access
- Coaching access
- [FACT] An athlete without access to specific training cannot develop certain capacities

**Equipment and Environment**
- Quality of training equipment
- Training environment (terrain, weather, altitude)
- [ASSUMPTION] Quality equipment allows better stimulus; poor equipment limits precision

### What It Is Not

- Not excuses (constraints are real; excuses are false claims)
- Not permanent (some constraints change with time; others are fixed)
- Not the athlete's fault (constraints are conditions to work within, not failures)
- Not destiny (constraints limit options; they don't determine outcome)

### Examples

- "Constraints: Previous ACL surgery (left knee sensitive to high impact), 45 years old (recovery slower, injury risk higher), 8 hours per week available for training, sleeps 6-7 hours most nights, high work stress (financial demands), no altitude training access."
- "Constraints: None significant. 28 years old, no injury history, 20 hours per week available, sleeps 8+ hours regularly, low external stress, full facility access."

### Anti-Examples

- "The athlete is limited" (limited at what? Be specific)
- "The athlete can't do hard training" (why not? State the specific constraint)

### Open Questions

- [QUESTION] How much should training be adapted to constraints vs. pushing the constraints? When does accommodation become enabling?
- [QUESTION] If an athlete has a constraint they could theoretically remove (sleep, for example), should Aurora recommend removing it?
- [QUESTION] How does Aurora prioritize when multiple constraints compete (limited time AND limited recovery)?

## Discovery Area 7: Context

### Definition

[FACT] The external environment in which training occurs. Context shapes what stimulus is appropriate, what recovery is possible, and what impact should be expected.

### What It Includes

**Training Cycle Phase**
- Off-season (transition, recovery)
- Base (building aerobic foundation)
- Build (developing specific capacity)
- Peak (sharpening toward competition)
- Taper (preparing for race)
- [FACT] The same workout means different things in different phases

**Competition Calendar**
- When are the athlete's target events?
- When are secondary competitions?
- [FACT] Competition calendar determines training structure; training serves competition, not vice versa

**Season (Climate/Weather)**
- What season is it (racing season, off-season)?
- What weather constraints apply (cold, heat, rain)?
- [FACT] Weather affects training load capacity, recovery, injury risk

**Life Events and Disruptions**
- Travel (disrupts sleep, routine, recovery)
- Illness or minor injury (requires adjustment)
- Major life events (wedding, move, job change)
- [FACT] Disruptions affect recovery capacity; training load should adapt

**Training Environment**
- Familiar vs. unfamiliar (unfamiliar requires adaptation)
- Equipment state (bike in good repair vs. needs maintenance)
- [FACT] Environment quality affects performance data quality and injury risk

**Team or Coach Dynamics**
- Training partners or team structure
- Coach strategy and philosophy
- [FACT] Social context affects compliance, effort, and sustainability

**Altitude and Environmental Stressors**
- Altitude training effects
- Heat adaptation demands
- [FACT] Environmental stress (altitude, heat) competes with training stress for recovery resources

### Examples

- "Context: Currently in build phase, targeting regional championship in 8 weeks. Weather: summer heat, morning training to avoid afternoon heat. Recent international travel (jet lag resolved 4 days ago). Familiar training routes. New training partner (increases motivation and social commitment). Coach emphasizes long rides; training philosophy: polarized."
- "Context: Currently in base phase, off-season, no target event for 12 weeks. Weather: winter, cold, short daylight (limits volume). Familiar indoor trainer setup. Solo training (athlete-driven). Coach emphasizes consistency over intensity."

### Anti-Examples

- "The weather is bad" (context, not excuse; adjust training accordingly)
- "I'm traveling" (context, not excuse; reduce training load accordingly)

### Open Questions

- [QUESTION] How much weight should Aurora give context vs. current data? If context says "reduce load" but athlete is fresh, which prevails?
- [QUESTION] Is context something Aurora observes, or something the athlete reports? How does Aurora know context has changed?
- [QUESTION] Should Aurora proactively adjust recommendations for known context, or wait until the athlete reports problems?

## Time: Classification of Every Concept

[FACT] Aurora reasons across time. Time is part of the model.

| Concept | Change Frequency | Implication |
|---|---|---|
| Sport | Never | Fixed throughout athlete's involvement in Aurora |
| Sex | Never | Fixed |
| Age | Slowly (1 year per year) | Changes systematically; affects recovery, adaptation, injury risk |
| Physiology Archetype | Never | Fixed; can be expressed differently but doesn't change |
| Competitive Category | Rarely (months/years) | Can shift (age-group to professional) but rare |
| Experience in Sport | Slowly accumulates | More experience, different adaptation |
| Competitive History | Continuously accumulates | Every race adds to history |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Current Objective (race) | Weekly (goal to goal) | Changes as races are completed or rescheduled |
| Training Phase | Weekly/monthly | Can shift rapidly with plan changes |
| Development Focus | Weekly/monthly | Can shift as priorities change |
| Long-term Vision | Rarely (months/years) | Generally stable; can shift with life changes |
| Why Training Matters | Rarely (years) | Life priorities shift slowly |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Acute Fatigue | Daily | Accumulates with training, clears with rest |
| Chronic Fatigue | Weekly | Emerges over weeks, clears over weeks |
| Recovery Status | Daily | Changes based on sleep, rest days, effort timing |
| Readiness | Daily | Fluctuates day-to-day |
| Confidence | Daily/hourly | Can shift rapidly (one bad effort, one success) |

| Adaptability | Relatively stable | Changes very slowly; largely determined by experience and physiology |

## Relationship: The Equation Reconsidered

**Statement**: Workout + Athlete = Impact

**Challenge**: This is incomplete. It misses essential dimensions.

**Improved Model**:

```
Impact = Workout × Athlete(Purpose, State, Capacity, History, Constraints, Context)
         at Moment,
         relative to Goals
```

What this means:

- **Workout**: The stimulus (duration, intensity, effort, content)
- **Athlete**: Not a single dimension but a multidimensional context
  - **Purpose**: Why this workout matters to this athlete
  - **State**: Readiness, fatigue, confidence, recovery status
  - **Capacity**: What this athlete can currently do
  - **History**: What this athlete has learned and experienced
  - **Constraints**: Limits on what adaptation is possible
  - **Context**: Environment, calendar, life circumstances
- **Moment**: The specific time (not generalizable; impacts vary by day)
- **Goals**: The outcome the athlete is pursuing (impact is meaningful only relative to purpose)

**Critical Relationships**:

1. The same workout produces different impact in different athletes → Athlete matters
2. The same athlete in different states produces different impact from the same workout → State matters
3. An athlete's history shapes how they respond to similar future stimulus → History matters
4. The purpose determines whether impact moves toward or away from the athlete's goal → Purpose matters
5. Constraints limit what impact is possible → Constraints matter
6. Context determines what stimulus is appropriate → Context matters

**Consequence for Aurora**:

Aurora cannot interpret a workout without understanding all seven dimensions of the athlete. Partial understanding produces partial (and wrong) interpretation.

## Unknowns: What Aurora Can Never Know

[ASSUMPTION] Aurora must explicitly acknowledge its limitations. These are permanent blindspots:

**Psychological State**
- The athlete's actual confidence (they can report it, but reporting is not certainty)
- Self-doubt, anxiety, fear
- Intrinsic motivation (why they really want to do this)
- [FACT] Behavior reveals something about psychology, but not everything

**Subjective Experience**
- How the workout felt (hard, easy, fun, painful, meaningful)
- Whether the athlete was present (mentally engaged) or checked out
- The athlete's own sense of what they learned from the workout
- [FACT] Athlete report is data, but it's not ground truth

**Compliance**
- Whether the athlete actually did the prescribed workout
- Whether they did it with honest effort or half-effort
- Whether they modified it and how
- [FACT] Device data shows something happened, but not whether it was what was prescribed

**Nutritional and Sleep Details**
- Exact sleep timing and quality (with or without sleep data)
- Nutritional intake and hydration
- Digestion and nutrient absorption
- [FACT] These affect recovery but are not directly observable

**Life Stress and Factors**
- Exact level of work stress, relationship stress, financial stress
- Family obligations and demands
- Internal health issues (inflammation, illness brewing)
- [FACT] External stress affects capacity but is invisible to training data

**Genetic Ceiling**
- The athlete's actual genetic potential in any domain
- Whether current capacity is close to ceiling or far from it
- Whether a plateau is due to genetics or due to training approach
- [FACT] Ceiling exists but is unknown

**Invisible Impact**
- Mental impact (confidence gained or lost)
- Technical impact (subtle movement improvements)
- Tactical impact (better race sense, improved decision-making)
- Psychological resilience (learned something about suffering)
- [FACT] These may matter more than physical impact but are hardest to measure

**Future Choices**
- Whether the athlete will actually commit to the program
- Whether they'll stay healthy
- Whether they'll face life disruption
- [FACT] Aurora cannot predict the athlete's future choices

## Final Reflection: The Essential Thing

**Question**: If Aurora forgot everything about an athlete except one thing, what would be the last thing it should forget?

**Answer**: **Purpose.**

**Why**:

An athlete without purpose is not an athlete. They're a person exercising.

Purpose is what transforms a workout from activity into training. Purpose is what makes impact meaningful. Purpose is what determines whether a change is progress or regression.

With purpose alone, Aurora can infer what it needs to know:

- If the athlete wants to race in 10 weeks, Aurora knows the time horizon and can infer the phase.
- If the purpose is endurance, Aurora knows which capacities matter.
- If the purpose is recovery, Aurora knows which stimulus is appropriate.
- If the purpose is development, Aurora can distinguish between "impact aligned with purpose" and "impact misaligned."

Without purpose, all the other data is noise.

State doesn't matter if there's no purpose toward which state is being optimized.

Capacity doesn't matter if there's no purpose that capacity serves.

History doesn't matter if there's no future direction the history informs.

Constraints don't matter if there's nothing we're constrained about.

But with purpose? Aurora can work backward from "what does this athlete want to become?" and forward from "what does this athlete need to do?"

**Corollary**: This is also why the first question Aurora should always ask is not "How are you?" but "What are you training for?"

Everything else follows.

## Hypotheses to Carry Forward

1. [HYPOTHESIS] The same athlete in the same state, pursuing different purposes, will experience different impact from the same workout.
2. [HYPOTHESIS] An athlete's previous experience with similar stimulus is more predictive of response than any single current metric.
3. [HYPOTHESIS] Constraints are not failures; they're conditions that require wisdom, not excuses.
4. [HYPOTHESIS] Confidence is as important to impact as physiology. A confident athlete in poor state may produce greater impact than an unconfident athlete in good state.
5. [HYPOTHESIS] History creates path dependency: an athlete who overcame overtraining will train differently forever after, even if data says they could tolerate more load.

---

## Non-Goals

What this model is NOT trying to do:

- Create a profile (this is not a personality test)
- Enable ranking or comparison (each athlete is unique; comparison is meaningless)
- Prescribe a training approach (knowing the athlete doesn't determine the training)
- Replace coaching judgment (the model supports judgment, it doesn't replace it)
- Capture everything about the athlete (only what affects training interpretation)
- Enable prediction (the model explains, it doesn't predict)

## Open Questions

Carried forward for Architecture Design:

1. [QUESTION] How does Aurora learn about each dimension of the athlete? Which require explicit reporting? Which can be inferred from behavior?
2. [QUESTION] When athlete's reported purpose conflicts with their behavior, which should Aurora trust?
3. [QUESTION] How should Aurora weight recent state vs. historical pattern? If an athlete has always been cautious but current data shows they're ready, which prevails?
4. [QUESTION] Can Aurora ever challenge an athlete's constraints, or must it work within them?
5. [QUESTION] How does Aurora represent uncertainty about the athlete? (E.g., "I think this athlete has X capacity, but I'm not sure")
6. [QUESTION] When the athlete changes (burns out on racing, gets injured, changes life priorities), how does Aurora recognize the shift and update its model?

- [UNKNOWN] Whether a single model of athlete fits all sports equally
