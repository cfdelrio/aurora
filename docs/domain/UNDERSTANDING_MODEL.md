# The Understanding Model — Conceptual Discovery

> How does Aurora become better at understanding a specific athlete over time?
>
> Not instantly. Progressively. Understanding matures — and it can also rot.

---

| Tag | Meaning |

| **[FACT]** | Well-established or self-evidently true. |
| **[HYPOTHESIS]** | Reasoned but unproven for our athletes. Must be validated. |

## The Question This Document Answers

The four prior discoveries built up to a single gap:

- **The Thesis** says *why* Aurora exists.
- **Training Impact** says *what* Aurora studies.
- **The Evidence Model** says *how* Aurora reasons about a single claim.
- **The Athlete Model** says *who* Aurora reasons about.

None of them answer this: **how does Aurora get better at a specific athlete over time?**

A coach who has worked with an athlete for three years knows things a coach on day one cannot. Not because the data is different — because the *understanding* is different. The veteran coach has seen this athlete fade, recover, surprise themselves, lie about their fatigue, break through. They have a model of *this person* that no first session could produce.

[ASSUMPTION] Aurora must earn the same thing the same way: slowly, through accumulated, tested, revised understanding. This document defines what that understanding is, how it grows, how it decays, and — most importantly — how Aurora can tell the difference between real understanding and overconfidence.

---

## First Principle: Understanding Is Never Binary

[FACT] Understanding does not move from `Unknown → Known`. It moves through stages:

```
   Unknown
      │
      ▼
   Thin Understanding        "I have barely met this athlete."
      │
      ▼
   Working Understanding     "I have hypotheses about this athlete, lightly held."
      │
      ▼
   Trusted Understanding     "I have patterns I have tested and that have held."
      │
      ▼
   Mature Understanding      "I understand how this athlete responds, and I know
                              the edges of what I understand."
```

[ASSUMPTION] These are not implementation states or database flags. They are conceptual stages of **Aurora's confidence in its own model of a specific athlete.** Progress between them is earned by tested evidence, not by elapsed time or accumulated volume. Six months of identical easy rides does not move Aurora past Thin Understanding of how the athlete responds to intensity.

---

## The Distinction That Makes This Model Necessary

[FACT] There are two completely different kinds of confidence, and conflating them is the central error this document exists to prevent.

| | Belongs to | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Confidence in a hypothesis** | The Evidence Model | "This workout likely produced fatigue. (78%)" |
| **Confidence in understanding** | This document | "Aurora has enough tested history to know how *this athlete* usually responds to this kind of workout." |

[FACT] The first is about a *claim*. The second is about Aurora's *competence regarding a person*.

They can diverge sharply, and the dangerous case is when they do:

- Aurora can be **highly confident in a single hypothesis** (the evidence for fatigue is strong and convergent) while having **thin understanding of the athlete** (it has never seen how this athlete recovers, so it cannot say what the fatigue *means* for them).
- [ASSUMPTION] A high-confidence claim built on thin understanding of the athlete is exactly the kind of claim Aurora should hedge — because the claim may be locally correct yet personally misleading.

This document governs the second column. The Evidence Model governs the first.

## Discovery Area 1: First Contact

**What can Aurora know after a single workout?**

[FACT] After one workout, Aurora knows what was *recorded* and what is *interpretable in general terms*. It can place the workout against population priors: "this looks like a hard threshold effort," "this duration and intensity usually produces meaningful fatigue."

**What must remain unknown after one workout?**

[FACT] Everything athlete-*specific*:
- How *this* athlete recovers from *this* stimulus.
- This athlete's individual lag between stimulus and visible response.
- Whether this effort was typical or exceptional for them.
- Whether the numbers mean what they'd mean for the average athlete.

[ASSUMPTION] One workout is a data point, not a pattern. A single observation cannot distinguish the athlete's normal from their exception, because Aurora has no normal to compare against yet.

**What should Aurora say after one workout?**

[ASSUMPTION] Aurora should speak in the language of population priors, openly labeled as such:
- *"This looks like a hard session. Athletes usually need a day or two to absorb this kind of load — but I don't know your pattern yet."*

**What should Aurora never say after one workout?**

[ASSUMPTION] Anything that claims personal knowledge it has not earned:
- ✗ *"This is a lot for you."* (Aurora has no "you" yet — no baseline.)
- ✗ *"You're overreaching."* (No chronic context exists.)
- ✗ *"You respond well to this."* (One sample cannot establish a response pattern.)

[FACT] The honest posture at First Contact is: *general knowledge, personal humility.*

### Open Questions
- [QUESTION] Is there a minimum number of workouts, or workout *types*, before Aurora may make any athlete-specific claim at all?
- [QUESTION] Should First Contact be per-athlete, or per-stimulus-type? (Aurora may have mature understanding of an athlete's endurance response and zero understanding of their response to intervals.)

## Discovery Area 2: Pattern Recognition

**When does repeated evidence become a pattern?**

[ASSUMPTION] A pattern is not "the same thing happened twice." A pattern is **a relationship that recurs under varying conditions and survives attempts to explain it away.**

**Is repetition enough?**

[FACT] No. Repetition under *identical* conditions can be a single confounded observation repeated, not an independent confirmation. Three hard weeks followed by three flat tests might be one phenomenon (accumulated fatigue) observed three times — not three independent confirmations of a response pattern.

**What makes a pattern meaningful?**

[HYPOTHESIS] A pattern earns trust when:
1. It recurs across *varied* conditions (different phases, states, contexts), not just identical ones.
2. It has a *plausible mechanism* (it agrees with what the Athlete Model and exercise physiology would predict), or, if it contradicts the mechanism, it is strong enough to demand attention.
3. It *predicts forward* — Aurora expected it before seeing it, and it held.
4. Attempts to explain it as artifact or coincidence have failed.

**How does Aurora distinguish coincidence, noise, artifact, and real pattern?**

[ASSUMPTION] These are four different failures of inference, and each has a different defense:

| Candidate | What it really is | Defense |
|---|---|---|
| **Coincidence** | Two unrelated things lined up by chance | Demand recurrence across varied conditions; chance rarely repeats under variation |
| **Noise** | Random fluctuation around a true value | Demand effect size beyond normal variability |
| **Artifact** | Measurement error (device, calibration) | Cross-check against independent signals; an artifact rarely corrupts all of them the same way |
| **Real pattern** | A genuine athlete-specific relationship | Survives all three challenges above and predicts forward |

[FACT] **Convergence beats repetition.** (Inherited from the Evidence Model.) Several independent signals agreeing once is stronger evidence of a pattern than one signal repeating many times.

### Open Questions
- [QUESTION] How many varied confirmations constitute a pattern? Is the threshold universal or per-dimension (physical patterns confirm faster than mental ones)?
- [QUESTION] How does Aurora avoid the trap where it *expects* a pattern (from priors) and then sees it in noise — confirmation bias in inference?

## Discovery Area 3: Trust

**When can Aurora trust its own understanding?**

[ASSUMPTION] Aurora may trust its understanding of an athlete *in a specific dimension* when its model of that dimension has made forward predictions that held, across varied conditions, and survived contradiction.

**What increases trust?**
- [HYPOTHESIS] Successful forward prediction (Aurora said what would happen, and it did).
- [HYPOTHESIS] Convergent, independent signals agreeing.
- [HYPOTHESIS] The athlete's own report confirming Aurora's inference ("yes, that block wrecked me").
- [HYPOTHESIS] Surviving a genuine test — a change of conditions that *could* have broken the model but didn't.

**What decreases trust?**
- [HYPOTHESIS] Surprise (covered next): the athlete did something the model didn't allow for.
- [HYPOTHESIS] The athlete changing (injury, life event, aging, new training history) without the model updating.
- [HYPOTHESIS] Time itself, in fast-changing dimensions — yesterday's readiness understanding is nearly worthless today.

**What makes understanding fragile?**
- [ASSUMPTION] Understanding built on few observations, identical conditions, or population priors dressed as personal knowledge is fragile. It looks like knowledge but has never been tested.

**What makes understanding durable?**
- [ASSUMPTION] Understanding that has been *challenged and held* is durable. Durability is not a function of confidence level — it is a function of how many ways the belief could have died and didn't.

[FACT] **Trust is earned by surviving challenge, not by accumulating agreement.** A belief that has only ever been confirmed has never been tested.

### Open Questions
- [QUESTION] Can trust in understanding be calibrated the way claim-confidence is — i.e., can Aurora check whether its "Trusted Understanding" athletes actually behave as predicted ~X% of the time?
- [QUESTION] Should trust be dimension-specific always, or is there a global "I know this athlete" that legitimately transfers across dimensions?

## Discovery Area 4: Surprise

**What happens when new evidence contradicts what Aurora believed?**

[FACT] Surprise is the single most informative event in the life of understanding. A confirmation tells Aurora little it didn't already believe. A surprise tells Aurora its model is wrong somewhere — and *where* is exactly what it needs to learn.

[ASSUMPTION] Aurora must never discard a surprise reflexively as noise. The reflex to protect a comfortable model from disconfirming evidence is the mechanism by which understanding goes stale.

**The conceptual response to surprise — a graded ladder, not a single reaction:**

[ASSUMPTION] When evidence contradicts the model, Aurora proceeds in this order:

1. **Check the evidence first.** Is the surprising observation an artifact (Evidence Model: observations are fallible by default)? A genuinely corrupt reading should not damage a good model. *But the burden of proof is on dismissal* — Aurora must show it's an artifact, not assume it.
2. **Lower confidence in the affected belief.** Even before knowing why, a real contradiction means the belief was less certain than Aurora thought. Confidence drops immediately.
3. **Reopen the hypothesis.** The belief returns from "knowledge" to "hypothesis under test." Aurora starts actively looking for what's going on.
4. **Revise the athlete model.** If the surprise recurs or converges with other signals, the model of the athlete was wrong, not the athlete. Update it. (E.g., "this athlete recovers faster than I thought.")
5. **Ask the athlete.** [ASSUMPTION] When the surprise touches something Aurora cannot observe — life stress, illness, a change in motivation — the honest move is to *ask*, not to invent an explanation. The athlete's voice resolves ambiguities the data cannot.

[HYPOTHESIS] The magnitude of the surprise should set how far up the ladder Aurora climbs. A small deviation lowers confidence slightly. A large, convergent, repeated contradiction should revise the model outright.

[FACT] One surprise is a question, not a verdict. A single contradiction reopens a belief; it does not by itself overturn a well-tested one. But it must never be ignored.

### Open Questions
- [QUESTION] How large must a surprise be before it revises the model rather than merely lowering confidence?
- [QUESTION] How does Aurora distinguish "the athlete changed" (model should update) from "the athlete had an off day" (model should hold, confidence dips temporarily)?
- [QUESTION] Should a resolved surprise be remembered as part of the athlete's history even after the model re-stabilizes? (Probably yes — it's evidence about how this athlete varies.)

## Discovery Area 5: Individuality

**When does Aurora stop relying on population knowledge and start relying on personal knowledge?**

[FACT] This transition is the core of Aurora's value. Anyone can apply population priors. The companion the thesis describes is worth having *because it understands the individual.*

[ASSUMPTION] The transition is **gradual, dimension-by-dimension, and evidence-gated** — not a switch that flips for the whole athlete at once.

```
   Early:   "Athletes often respond this way."        (population prior — a belief)
              │  accumulate tested, athlete-specific evidence
              ▼
   Blended: "Athletes often respond this way, and
             so far you seem to as well."             (prior + thin personal confirmation)
              │  personal evidence accumulates and survives challenge
              ▼
   Late:    "You usually respond this way —
             which is different from most athletes."   (personal knowledge — earned)
```

[ASSUMPTION] The crucial rule, inherited from the Evidence Model: **priors must stay visible.** A population belief must never silently become "I know this about you." Aurora should always be able to say which of its beliefs about an athlete are personal (earned from this athlete) and which are borrowed from the population.

[HYPOTHESIS] The most valuable understanding is precisely where the individual *departs* from the population — "you recover faster than the model expects," "intervals do less for you than for most." These departures are where Aurora beats a generic plan, and they are only discoverable once personal evidence overrides the prior.

[FACT] Population priors never fully disappear. In dimensions where personal evidence is thin (a stimulus type the athlete has never done), Aurora should fall back to priors honestly, even for an athlete it otherwise understands deeply.

### Open Questions
- [QUESTION] How much athlete-specific evidence is enough to override a population prior in a given dimension?
- [QUESTION] When personal knowledge and population prior conflict early (athlete seems to defy the textbook on 2 samples), which does Aurora lead with, and how does it hedge?

## Discovery Area 6: Forgetting

**Should Aurora forget?**

[ASSUMPTION] Yes — selectively. A model that weighs a three-year-old fitness test as heavily as last week's is not wise; it is stale. But forgetting is dangerous: some things must never be lost. The question is *what* decays, *what* persists, and *what* is archived but set aside.

[ASSUMPTION] Three fates for what Aurora knows:

| Fate | Meaning | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Persist (never forget)** | Permanently shapes interpretation | Purpose, serious injury history, overtraining episodes, identity (sport, physiology archetype), breakthrough moments |
| **Decay (fade with time)** | Recent matters more; old matters less | Capacity estimates (an FTP from a year ago), recent state, current readiness, seasonal context |
| **Archive (keep, don't actively use)** | Out of the active model, but recoverable | Old races, completed training cycles, resolved minor injuries, superseded capacity estimates, last season's context |

[FACT] **State decays fastest, identity never.** This maps directly onto the Athlete Model's time classification: what changes hourly (readiness, soreness) should be weighted by recency and decay quickly; what never changes (sport, physiology archetype, purpose) should persist.

[ASSUMPTION] **Injuries and overtraining episodes must never fully decay**, even when old. They permanently change the interpretation of future load — an athlete who once overtrained on high volume carries that fact forever, even if current data says they could tolerate more. (This is the path-dependency hypothesis from the Athlete Model.)

[ASSUMPTION] Archived knowledge is not deleted. A resolved injury from three years ago should not actively constrain today's plan, but if a similar pain reappears, Aurora should be able to *recall* it. Forgetting-into-archive preserves the ability to recognize recurrence.

[HYPOTHESIS] Decay should be *graceful and reversible*, not a cliff. An old capacity estimate should lose weight gradually and be instantly restored to relevance if new evidence reconfirms it.

### Open Questions
- [QUESTION] At what rate should each kind of knowledge decay? Is decay rate the inverse of the Athlete Model's change-frequency table?
- [QUESTION] When an athlete returns after a long layoff, how much of the old model is still valid, and how much should snap back to Thin Understanding?
- [QUESTION] Can forgetting itself be a failure mode — Aurora archiving something it should have kept active? (Yes; see Failure Modes.)

## Discovery Area 7: Understanding Confidence

[FACT] This is the central deliverable: a measure of **how well Aurora understands an athlete**, distinct from how confident it is in any single claim.

[ASSUMPTION] Proposed levels (precise definitions, not labels):

| Level | Definition | What Aurora may do |
|---|---|---|
| **Unknown** | No relevant evidence about this athlete in this dimension. | Use population priors only; say so. |
| **Thin Understanding** | A few observations, untested, conditions not varied. | Offer prior-based suggestions, hedged; make no personal claims. |
| **Working Understanding** | Athlete-specific hypotheses exist, lightly held, partially confirmed. | Offer personal observations *as hypotheses*, with falsifiers attached. |
| **Trusted Understanding** | Patterns tested across varied conditions; forward predictions have held; survived contradiction. | Make personal claims with stated confidence; lead with personal knowledge over priors. |
| **Mature Understanding** | Trusted *and* Aurora knows the edges of its own model — where it's reliable and where it isn't. | Act as a genuine companion in this dimension; also name explicitly what it still doesn't understand. |

[FACT] **Understanding confidence is per-dimension, not global.** Aurora can have Mature Understanding of an athlete's aerobic response and Unknown understanding of their response to heat, simultaneously. A single global "I know this athlete" number would be a lie.

**How is it different from confidence in a single claim?**

[FACT] Claim-confidence answers *"how sure am I this is true?"* Understanding-confidence answers *"how qualified am I to even be making claims about this person in this area?"* The second bounds the first: [ASSUMPTION] a claim's effective trustworthiness is capped by the understanding behind it. High claim-confidence under Thin Understanding should be reported with extra humility, because the model generating the claim is itself unproven.

### Open Questions
- [QUESTION] Can understanding confidence be calibrated against outcomes — do "Trusted Understanding" dimensions actually predict correctly more often than "Working" ones?
- [QUESTION] Should understanding confidence ever *cap* the confidence Aurora is allowed to report on a claim, as a hard rule?

## Discovery Area 8: Limits

**What should Aurora explicitly admit it does not understand?**

[ASSUMPTION] Aurora must maintain a standing, visible account of its own ignorance — the same discipline as the Evidence Model's "DOES NOT KNOW," but applied to its *understanding of the person*:

- The athlete's inner experience — how training actually felt, what it meant to them.
- Dimensions with no data trace (much of mental, technical, tactical capacity).
- Anything happening in the athlete's life that Aurora cannot observe.
- Whether a plateau is the athlete's ceiling or Aurora's failure to find the right stimulus.
- The future — Aurora understands patterns, it does not foresee choices.

**How should uncertainty be communicated?**

[ASSUMPTION] Honestly and specifically. Not generic disclaimers, but located humility: *"I understand how you handle volume; I don't yet understand how you handle high heat — we've never trained in it."* Specific humility is trustworthy; vague hedging is noise.

**What kinds of athlete reality are invisible to Aurora?**

[FACT] (Inherited from the Athlete Model's Unknowns.) Subjective experience, true compliance, life stress, nutrition, sleep quality, genetic ceiling, and most invisible-dimension impact. These are not gaps to be closed with more data — they are permanent boundaries of the system.

**What kind of humility should Aurora maintain?**

[ASSUMPTION] The humility of a good coach who has worked with someone for years and *still* says "I might be wrong about you." Long understanding should deepen humility, not replace it — because the better Aurora knows an athlete, the more its confident errors would cost.

### Open Questions
- [QUESTION] How does Aurora keep its limits *visible to the athlete* without undermining trust in what it does understand?
- [QUESTION] Are there dimensions where Aurora should refuse to claim understanding at all, no matter how much data accumulates?

## Relationship to the Evidence Model

[FACT] The Evidence Model and the Understanding Model are two layers of the same epistemology.

- The **Evidence Model** operates on a **single claim**: observation → signal → evidence → hypothesis → knowledge → confidence. It answers "is *this* true, and how sure am I?"
- The **Understanding Model** operates on **accumulated claims about one athlete over time**. It answers "how good is my model of *this person*?"

[ASSUMPTION] Each tested claim from the Evidence Model is an input to the Understanding Model. Many claims, confirmed and contradicted over time, are what raise or lower understanding confidence. The Understanding Model is the Evidence Model integrated over time and over one person.

[FACT] They share their core values: defeasibility (all understanding is revisable), traceability (understanding must be traceable to the claims that built it), calibration (understanding confidence, like claim confidence, must mean what it says), and the primacy of surprise (contradiction is more informative than confirmation).

---

## Relationship to the Athlete Model

[FACT] The Athlete Model defines the **dimensions** Aurora understands across (Identity, Purpose, State, Capacity, History, Constraints, Context) and *how fast each changes*. The Understanding Model defines **how well Aurora knows the athlete within each dimension** and *how that knowledge matures and decays.*

[ASSUMPTION] The two compose precisely:
- The Athlete Model's **time-classification table** (what changes never / slowly / daily / hourly) *is* the Understanding Model's **decay schedule**. Fast-changing dimensions support only fast-expiring understanding; stable dimensions can accumulate durable understanding.
- The Athlete Model's **Unknowns** are the Understanding Model's permanent **Limits**.
- The Athlete Model's **path-dependency hypothesis** (history permanently shapes response) is why some understanding must **persist and never decay**.

[HYPOTHESIS] Understanding confidence should be tracked per Athlete-Model-dimension. "Mature Understanding of Capacity, Thin Understanding of Constraints" is a meaningful, actionable state.

## The Relationship Chain, Challenged

**Proposed chain:** Observation → Evidence → Understanding → Decision Support

[ASSUMPTION] This is incomplete in three ways:

1. **It omits the athlete's voice.** Observation is device data; the athlete's report is a separate, irreplaceable input that enters at evidence *and* resolves surprise. It is not derived from observation.
2. **It omits purpose and context.** Evidence becomes *understanding of impact* only relative to purpose (from the Athlete Model). Strip purpose out and "understanding" has nothing to be about.
3. **It is drawn as one-directional, but understanding feeds back.** Understanding shapes how the *next* observation is interpreted. A surprise revises understanding, which revises the interpretation of future evidence. It is a loop, not an arrow.

**Improved model:**

```
        ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
        │                                                      │
        ▼                                                      │
   OBSERVATION ──┐                                             │
                 ├──► EVIDENCE ──► UNDERSTANDING ──► DECISION  │
   ATHLETE       │   (relative     (of THIS athlete,  SUPPORT  │
   REPORT     ───┤    to a          maturing &                 │
                 │    hypothesis)   decaying                   │
   PURPOSE  +    │                  over TIME)                 │
   CONTEXT    ───┘                       │                     │
                                         │ surprise / revision │
                                         └─────────────────────┘
                                          feeds back to reshape
                                          interpretation of the
                                          next observation
```

[FACT] Understanding is not a stage data passes through once. It is a standing model that every new observation updates and that, in turn, conditions how every new observation is read.

## Failure Modes

[ASSUMPTION] Each failure mode is a specific way understanding goes wrong, with a named defense.

### 1. Overconfidence
Aurora believes it understands the athlete more than the evidence warrants.
- **Mechanism:** Treating accumulated agreement as proof; never testing the belief.
- **Defense:** Understanding confidence must be earned by *survived challenge*, not volume of confirmation. Calibrate it against outcomes.

### 2. Premature Personalization
Aurora draws athlete-specific conclusions from too little evidence.
- **Mechanism:** One or two samples mistaken for a pattern; a prior dressed up as personal knowledge.
- **Defense:** Patterns require varied conditions and forward prediction (Area 2). Keep priors visible (Area 5). Stay at Thin Understanding until evidence earns the next level.

### 3. Stale Understanding
Aurora keeps applying old patterns after the athlete has changed.
- **Mechanism:** No decay; old data weighted equally with new; ignoring surprise.
- **Defense:** Decay schedule tied to the Athlete Model's change-frequency table (Area 6). Treat surprise as a signal the model may be expired (Area 4).

### 4. False Pattern Recognition
Aurora mistakes noise, coincidence, or artifact for a real personal response pattern.
- **Mechanism:** Confirmation bias — expecting a pattern from priors, then "seeing" it in randomness.
- **Defense:** The four-way discrimination of Area 2 (coincidence/noise/artifact/pattern). Demand effect size, cross-signal convergence, and survival of explain-away attempts.

### 5. Ignoring the Athlete's Voice
Aurora trusts device data over the athlete's lived experience when it shouldn't.
- **Mechanism:** Treating observation as ground truth; forgetting that much of athlete reality is invisible (Area 8).
- **Defense:** The athlete's report is a first-class input (Relationship Chain). When data and report conflict on something only the athlete can know, the report wins or, at minimum, triggers a question (Area 4, step 5).

### 6. Excessive Humility
Aurora refuses to help even when evidence and understanding are strong enough.
- **Mechanism:** Over-correcting for false-positive fear; hedging everything into uselessness.
- **Defense:** Mature Understanding *licenses* confident, personal support. Humility means knowing the edges of the model — not refusing to act inside them. A companion who never commits is as useless as one who is always wrong.

[HYPOTHESIS] Failure modes 1–4 are errors of *too much* understanding claimed; 5 is an error of *source*; 6 is the error of *too little* understanding acted on. A healthy Aurora sits between premature confidence and paralytic doubt — and the whole point of measuring understanding confidence is to locate that middle honestly.

## Candidate Language

Terms this discovery proposes for Aurora's ubiquitous language:

- **Understanding** — Aurora's model of a *specific athlete*, distinct from any single claim.
- **Understanding Confidence** — calibrated degree to which Aurora's model of an athlete (in a dimension) is trustworthy. Levels: Unknown, Thin, Working, Trusted, Mature.
- **First Contact** — the state of having data but no athlete-specific understanding.
- **Pattern** — a relationship that recurs under *varied* conditions and survives explain-away.
- **Surprise** — evidence that contradicts the current model; the most informative event.
- **Personalization** — the evidence-gated transition from population priors to personal knowledge.
- **Decay** — the deliberate, graceful loss of weight of time-sensitive understanding.
- **Archive** — knowledge kept but removed from the active model, recoverable on recurrence.
- **Path-dependent memory** — understanding (injury, overtraining) that must never decay.

## Hypotheses to Carry Forward

1. [HYPOTHESIS] Understanding confidence can be calibrated against outcomes — "Trusted" dimensions will predict correctly more often than "Working" ones, measurably.
2. [HYPOTHESIS] The most valuable understanding is where the individual departs from population priors; these departures are Aurora's edge over a generic plan.
3. [HYPOTHESIS] Surprise, handled well, accelerates understanding faster than confirmation — a well-handled contradiction teaches more than ten agreements.
4. [HYPOTHESIS] Decay rates are the inverse of the Athlete Model's change-frequency table; what changes fast supports only short-lived understanding.
5. [HYPOTHESIS] Capping claim-confidence by understanding-confidence will make Aurora more trusted than a system that reports claim-confidence alone.

---

## Non-Goals

What this model is **not** trying to do:

- Not a learning algorithm, training procedure, or statistical method (this is domain, not ML).
- Not a data-retention or storage policy (forgetting here is conceptual weighting, not deletion mechanics).
- Not a replacement for the athlete's self-knowledge (understanding supports the athlete; it does not overrule their lived experience).
- Not a claim that enough data yields complete understanding (the Limits are permanent).
- Not a global "athlete score" (understanding is per-dimension and defeasible).
- Not prediction (understanding explains how an athlete responds; it does not foretell their choices).

## Final Reflection

> **What is the biggest mistake Aurora could make when believing it understands an athlete?**

[ASSUMPTION] The biggest mistake is **mistaking the population for the person** — taking a confident, well-evidenced claim that is *true on average* and presenting it as *true of this athlete*, when Aurora has not actually earned personal understanding.

This is the most dangerous error precisely because it does not feel like ignorance. It feels like competence. The evidence is real. The claim is statistically sound. The confidence number is honest *about the claim*. And yet the understanding behind it is borrowed, not earned — and the athlete is treated as an instance of a category rather than as themselves.

It is dangerous for three reasons:

1. **It is invisible from the inside.** Overconfidence in a hypothesis announces itself when the hypothesis fails. But population-knowledge-mistaken-for-personal-knowledge keeps being *roughly right* — right often enough to never trigger alarm, wrong exactly when the athlete is exceptional, which is exactly when they needed Aurora most.
2. **It betrays the thesis.** Aurora's entire reason to exist is that it understands the *individual*. The moment it quietly substitutes the average athlete for the real one, it becomes the generic dashboard the thesis rejected — while still sounding personal.
3. **It silences the athlete's exceptions.** The athlete who recovers faster than the textbook, the one for whom intervals do nothing, the one whose fatigue hides — these are the people a generic model fails, and they are the people a true companion exists to serve.

[ASSUMPTION] The defense is this document's whole purpose: **keep understanding confidence separate from claim confidence, keep priors visible, and treat every surprise as a gift rather than a nuisance.** A surprise is the athlete telling Aurora "I am not the average — pay attention to *me*." Aurora's job is to listen.

## Success Criterion

> **"How do I know whether I truly understand this athlete, or am simply overconfident?"**

[ASSUMPTION] Aurora can now answer:

*I truly understand this athlete, in a given dimension, to the degree that my model of them has made forward predictions that held across varied conditions, has survived contradiction rather than merely collected agreement, rests on this athlete's own evidence rather than borrowed population priors — and to the degree that I can name exactly where that understanding ends. If my confidence rests on volume of agreement, identical conditions, untested priors, or has no stated edges — then I am not understanding. I am overconfident, and I should say so.*

The difference between understanding and overconfidence is not how sure Aurora feels. It is **how many ways the belief could have died, and didn't.**
